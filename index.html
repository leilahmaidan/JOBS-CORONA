As Coronavirus Spreads Globally, Manufacturing Companies play the Wait and See Game on Jobs

The global spread of coronavirus has left U.S. manufacturers standing in between a widening supply and demand fault line. The latest sign that companies are beginning to run into trouble.

By Laila Hmaidan
Published Sept. 11, 2019 8:00 PM

The manufacturing sector will experience a slump due to the impact of COVID-19. Of the Fortune 1000 companies surveyed by the American Chamber of Commerce Shanghai, 94% are experiencing disruption of their supply chain. As a result, impacted companies will not reach their 2020 tier annual sales target and the effects will be seen in layoffs and reduced wages.

According to the Institute for Supply Management’s Report On Business, an index that monitors monthly changes in manufacturer’s production levels, February’s backlog orders spiked by 4.6% from the previous month, the highest since April of last year. Even though the sector continued to experience a decrease in unemployment, it was not the case for durable goods, which was up by .4% from January. 2018 midterms.

While China has begun to see a decrease in new coronavirus cases, the country is not back to normal. In a typical year, there would be a high spike in production before and after the Chinese New Year to offset the slow down or halt in production during the holiday. Qais Alkurdi, owner of SirCom, an American manufacturer of early warning systems, is three weeks behind on inventory deliveries. Approximately 70% of products that were supposed to arrive from Germany in the last week of February and first week of March, never came. The company still has no expected delivery date.

“Our supply chain has been disrupted in every possible scenario, including from shipping and deliveries. The UPS and DHLs of the world are acting similar to how you see them around Christmas time, they are doing what they can but being pushed and pulled in every direction. The delays are bad and there is no communication from our logistics partners. We expect deliveries one day, we get them the next,” said Alkurdi.

SirCom, which assembles a majority of its product in Germany and the U.S., is experiencing disruption because it relies on key raw materials from China. The delays mean they will not reach their 2020 projected profits, even with a recovery in the next 2-3 weeks.

The company attempted to mitigate inventory gaps by seeking raw materials from other countries but were only successful in substituting 30% of the materials that came from China. With demand also coming from other manufacturers seeking alternatives, there was not enough supply.

Key materials required for lithium batteries and printed circuit boards, such as electronic circuitry, resistors and capacitors, were not easy to replace.

“We haven't yet been in a situation where we failed to meet [client] demand but we are at high risk, there is about a 65% chance we will not make our mid-April deadlines,” said Alkurdi.

For Alkurdi’s company, if supply chain issues continued into mid-April, the outcome would be catastrophic. Large projects that had been awarded to them would fail to meet their dates and the impact could be felt in jobs.
